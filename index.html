Every character multiplies the work

A brute-force attack tries every possible combination. Each character you add doesn't just add to the difficulty — it multiplies it by the size of your character pool. Add one symbol to a lowercase password and the attacker's job gets far bigger.

With a full keyboard (95 characters), each extra character makes brute-forcing roughly 95× longer. Going from 8 to 11 characters is about 857,000× harder — not 3× harder.

Why the world is moving to passphrases

Length beats complexity. A long string of ordinary words is easier for you to remember and harder for a machine to crack than a short scramble of symbols.

correct-horse-battery-staple — 28 characters, trivial to remember, and would take a modern offline cracker an astronomically long time. Compare that to P@ss1!, which falls in seconds.

Modern guidance from NIST now encourages length, discourages forced periodic resets, and drops the old "must contain a symbol" rules in favor of longer passphrases checked against known-breached lists. Four or five random words, a password manager, and unique passwords per site is the practical winning combination.

Your public IP is visible to every website, email server, and online game you connect to. On its own it's not a password — but combined with other data it can reveal your rough location and ISP, and it's what attackers probe when scanning for open ports.

What someone can — and can't — do with it

They generally can

See your approximate city and ISP, attempt to scan your network for exposed services, target you with denial-of-service traffic, and correlate your activity across sites that log IPs.

They generally cannot

Read your messages, get your home address from the IP alone, or log into your accounts. The IP is a starting point for an attacker, not a skeleton key.

Lower your exposure: use a reputable VPN on public Wi-Fi, keep your router firmware updated, disable UPnP and port-forwarding you don't need, and turn on your firewall. For a business, put guest Wi-Fi on a separate network from your point-of-sale and back-office machines.
Diagnostic 03

Defenses that actually earn their keep

Antivirus is one layer, not a force field. These are well-regarded options — but the habits below them matter more than any single product.

Reputable security software

A starting shortlist for individuals and small businesses. Test independently (AV-TEST, AV-Comparatives) before standardizing on one.

1

Bitdefender

Consistently top independent detection scores with light system impact. Strong small-business tier.

individual + business
2

Microsoft Defender

Built into Windows, free, and genuinely competitive now. For many users it's enough on its own.

free / built-in
3

Malwarebytes

Excellent as a second-opinion scanner and at cleaning active infections. Pairs well with Defender.

cleanup specialist
4

ESET

Lightweight, low false-positives, favored on older or lower-spec machines. Solid endpoint management.

low overhead
5

1Password / Bitwarden

Not antivirus — a password manager. Arguably does more for your security than any AV. Bitwarden has a free tier.

password manager

Avoid pirated or "free key" versions of paid security tools — they're a classic malware delivery method. Buy direct from the vendor.

The habits that beat any product

Turn on automatic updates, use a password manager with unique passwords, enable two-factor authentication everywhere it's offered, keep offline backups of anything you can't lose, and learn to spot phishing — most breaches start with a click, not a hack.
